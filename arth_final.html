TERMS

  • Stoicism: an ancient Greek school of philosophy founded in Athens that values indifference to the vicissitudes of fortune and to pleasure and pain
  • Etruscan: a period (ca. 800-200 BCE) characterized by engaging and stylized depictions of the human form and monumental religious and funerary architecture influenced by ancient Greece as well as the extensive use of terracotta
  • terracotta: hard-baked clay used for sculpture and as a building material that may be glazed or painted
  • Ancient Roman: a period (ca. 753 BCE-476 CE) characterized by the synthesis of earlier Greek and Etruscan models, naturalism, and monumental civic architecture as well as stylistic evolutions that coincide with political and religious transformations
  • republic: a form of government in which a state is ruled by representatives of the citizen body
  • empire: a major political unit having a territory of great extent or a number of territories under a single sovereign authority, known as an emperor/empress
  • engaged column: a column that is not freestanding but attached to a wall
  • portico: a porch or entrance to a building consisting of a covered and often columned area
  • concrete: a composite material composed of fine and coarse aggregate bonded together with a fluid cement that cures over time
  • verism: an artistic approach in which artists concern themselves with describing the exteri- or likeness of an object or person, usually by rendering its visible details in a finely executed, meticulous manner
  • mosaic: an image or pattern produced by arranging together small colored pieces of hard material, such as stone, tile, or glass
  • tessera(e): small piece(s) of stone, glass, or other object that is pieced together with many others to create a mosaic
  • foreshortening: a visual effect in which an object is shortened and turned deeper towards the picture plane to give the effect of receding in space
  • trompe l'oeil: a visual illusion in art, especially as used to trick the eye into perceiving a painted detail as a three-dimensional object
  • mystery cult: a religious group characterized by rites which were secret except to duly initiated worshippers
  • allegory/allegorical figure: a representation in which a character, place, or event can be interpreted to represent a hidden meaning with moral or political significance
  • pilaster: a rectangular column, especially one projecting from a wall
  • arcuation: an architectural mode characterized by the use of arches and vaults
  • voussoir(s): wedge shaped blocks holding the curve of the arch
  • keystone: a central stone at the summit of an arch
  • arch: a curved symmetrical structure spanning an opening and typically sup- porting the weight of a bridge, roof, or wall above it
  • barrel vault: the simplest form of vault consisting of an unbroken series of arches
  • groin vault: a form of fault produced by the intersection at right angles of two vaults
  • dome: an arch spun 180 degrees around its central axis
  • oculus: the round central opening of a dome
  • coffer: a square or polygonal sunken panel used for a ceiling or vault
  • tetrarchy: a government by four persons ruling jointly
  • nave: the central area of a basilica
  • apse: A recess, usually semicircular, in the wall of a basilica
  • aisle(s): portion(s) of a basilica that parallels or encircles the major sections of the structure
  • Chi Rho: a monogram of chi (ยง)and rho (ยก)as the first two letters of the Greek term Christos (Christ) or "anointed one
  • lunette: a semicircular space, sometimes over a doorway or under an arch, that contains decoration
  • baptistry: the place where baptisms take place
  • orant figure: a figure with its hands raised in prayer
  • relic: an object considered holy because it belonged to or was touched by a saint or other holy person
  • transept: the arm of a cruciform church perpendicular to the nave
  • clerestory: a row of windows in the upper part of a wall
  • pendentive: a curved triangle of vaulting formed by the intersection of a dome with its supporting arches
  • ambulatory: a covered walkway or corridor
  • Eucharist: the Christian ceremony commemorating the Last Supper, in which bread and wine are consecrated and consumed.
  • icon: a representation or image of a sacred personage, often considered sacred itself, used as an devotional object or aid
  • iconoclasm: the opposition to or destruction of images, especially those set up for religious veneration in the belief that such images represent idol worship
  • encaustic: an artistic technique in which pigment is mixed with melted wax and applied to a surface while the mixture is hot
  • pilgrimage: a journey to a place considered sacred for religious purposes
  • aniconism: the absence of artistic representations of the natural and supernat- ural worlds or of certain figures in religions
  • mosque: a Muslim place of worship
  • mirhab: a niche in the qibla wall of a mosque that indicates the direction of Mecca
  • minaret: a tall slender tower, typically part of a mosque, with a balcony from which a muezzin calls Muslims to prayer
  • Early Medieval European: a period (ca. 500-1000) characterized by stylized forms, elaborate patterns, intricate metalworking, bright colors, Christian themes, and continued references to classical antiquity
  • illuminated manuscript: a handwritten book decorated with bright colors and precious metals
  • mandorla: an almond-shaped halo surrounding the figure of Christ or other sacred figure
  • Romanesque: a period (ca. 1000-1200) with architecture characterized by round arches, great stone portals, rich decoration, and massive stone walls
  • reliquary: a container for holy relics
  • radiating chapels: a series of small chapels for the display of relics, which were arranged around an ambulatory
  • westwork: a monumental, often west-facing entrance section of a church
  • tympanum: the space enclosed by a lintel and an arch over a doorwa
  • Last Judgment: the judgment of humankind expected to take place at the end of the world, during which Jesus presides as judge according to Christian belief
  • Gothic: a period (ca. 1150-1500) with architecture characterized by ribbed vaults, stained glass windows, flying buttresses, pointed arches, and tall spires
  • stained glass: colored glass used for windows or decorative applications
  • rose window: a circular stained-glass window
  • round(ed) arch: an arch formed in a continuous curve; characteristic of Roman architecture
  • flying buttress: a buttress that stands apart from the main structure and con- nected to it by an arch
